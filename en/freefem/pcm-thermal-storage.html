Overview

This page documents a FreeFEM model developed to study thermal storage using phase change materials (PCM). The focus is on representing transient heat transfer, latent heat effects, and the evolution of the thermal response under realistic conditions.

The case is presented not only as a set of simulation results, but as a structured numerical workflow including formulation, implementation, assumptions, and interpretation.

Engineering Context

Phase change materials are relevant for thermal regulation and energy storage because they can absorb and release significant amounts of energy during melting and solidification. In practical systems, the main engineering challenge is to understand how geometry, thermal boundary conditions, and material properties affect storage performance.

In this case, the objective is to characterize the transient thermal behavior of a PCM-based storage configuration and identify the mechanisms that govern heat accumulation and phase change.

Problem Definition

The model considers a transient heat transfer problem in a system composed of a PCM region and solid conductive components. The main quantities of interest include:

  • Temperature evolution in time
  • Stored thermal energy
  • Heat accumulation rate
  • Influence of geometric or operating parameters

The problem may be extended to compare multiple configurations, such as changes in the number of fins, tube arrangement, or boundary-condition intensity.

Geometry and Domains

The computational domain is divided into at least two main regions:

  • PCM domain: region where phase change and thermal storage occur
  • Solid domain: tube walls, fins, or conductive support regions

Interface treatment between these regions is an important part of the model, since the thermal exchange across the common boundary strongly influences the transient response.

Governing Model

The problem is modeled through transient heat conduction with phase change effects. Depending on the final implementation, the latent heat contribution may be introduced through an apparent heat capacity approach, an enthalpy formulation, or an equivalent nonlinear representation.
