Overview

This page documents a FreeFEM-based model intended to study lithium-ion battery behavior from a finite element perspective, with emphasis on thermal response, transport mechanisms, and numerical implementation.

The purpose is not only to present simulation outputs, but also to expose the structure of the model: assumptions, domain decomposition, governing equations, numerical strategy, and interpretation of results.

Engineering Context

Lithium-ion batteries are governed by coupled physical processes involving charge transport, heat generation, diffusion, and material-dependent behavior. From an engineering perspective, modeling these systems is useful for understanding performance, temperature rise, safety margins, and the effect of operating conditions.

Depending on the scope of the case, the analysis may focus on thermal behavior alone, or on a more coupled description involving electrochemical and transport effects.

Problem Definition

The objective of the model is to represent one or more relevant aspects of lithium-ion battery behavior under transient operating conditions. Typical quantities of interest may include:

  • Temperature evolution in time
  • Internal heat generation
  • Potential or concentration fields
  • Effect of material or geometric parameters
  • Response under charge or discharge conditions

This template can later be adapted depending on whether the case is mainly thermal, electrochemical, or multiphysics.

Geometry and Domains

In the NTGK-oriented configuration considered here, the cylindrical 26650 cell is represented through a reduced multi-domain description composed of three main parts: the positive tab, the negative tab, and the active zone (jelly-roll). In this framework, the shell thermal resistance is considered negligible and is therefore not modeled explicitly.

This decomposition is convenient because it preserves the electrothermal structure required by the dual-potential formulation while remaining simpler than a fully resolved electrochemical description of all internal layers. For a first documented implementation in FreeFEM, this provides a clear starting point for defining regions, material properties, and coupled source terms.

Modeling note: the geometry documented here follows the same physical partition commonly used in NTGK implementations, where the electrothermal coupling is resolved over an active zone and current collectors.

Mathematical Model

The present case is formulated based on the Newman–Tiedemann–Gu–Kim (NTGK) model, adopted here as a reduced electrothermal framework suitable for predicting temperature evolution, current transfer, and voltage response in a cylindrical lithium-ion cell.

Within this approach, the battery is described by a coupled thermal field and two electric potential fields associated with the positive and negative current-collecting regions. The electrochemical coupling is introduced through the volumetric current transfer rate and the electrochemical heat source.

Thermal and electrical fields

The thermal behavior is governed by , while the electric potentials in the positive and negative domains are described by and , respectively.

\[ \frac{\partial (\rho C_p T)}{\partial t} - \nabla \cdot (k_c \nabla T) = \sigma_{pos} |\nabla \phi_{pos}|^2 + \sigma_{neg} |\nabla \phi_{neg}|^2 + q_{ech} \]
\[ \nabla \cdot (\sigma_{pos}\nabla \phi_{pos}) = -j \]
\[ \nabla \cdot (\sigma_{neg}\nabla \phi_{neg}) = j \]

Here, \(T\) is the temperature, \(k_c\) is the effective thermal conductivity, \(\phi_{pos}\) and \(\phi_{neg}\) are the electric potentials, \(\sigma_{pos}\) and \(\sigma_{neg}\) are the electrical conductivities, and \(j\) is the volumetric current transfer rate. The thermal conductivity \(k_c\) is treated as an anisotropic tensor \( \mathbf{k} = (k_r, k_{\theta}, k_z) \), reflecting the layered structure of the jelly-roll.

Current transfer rate

The volumetric current transfer rate is defined by .

\[ j = \frac{C_N}{C_{ref}\,Vol\,Y} \left[ U - \left(\phi_{pos} - \phi_{neg}\right) \right] \]

In , \(C_N\) denotes the nominal capacity of the cell, \(C_{ref}\) is a reference capacity, \(Vol\) is the volume of the active region, \(U\) is the open-circuit voltage, and \(Y\) is a function associated with electrochemical kinetics and charge-transfer resistance.

This term acts as the source term in the electric potential equations and , coupling the thermal and electrical fields.

NTGK closure functions

The NTGK closure is defined through the functions and , which depend on the depth of discharge \(DoD\) and temperature \(T\), where \(DoD = 1 - SOC\).

\[ U = \left(\sum_{n=0}^{5} a_n (DoD)^n \right) - C_2 (T - T_{ref}) \]
\[ Y = \left(\sum_{n=0}^{5} b_n (DoD)^n \right) \exp\left[-C_1 \left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_{ref}}\right)\right] \]

In and , the coefficients \(a_n\) and \(b_n\) are obtained through parameter estimation from experimental discharge data, while \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are empirical constants that account for temperature dependence. These functional forms describe the dependence of the open-circuit voltage and the current transfer modulation on both state of charge and temperature.

Electrochemical heat source

The electrochemical heat generation is defined by .

\[ q_{ech} = j \left[ U - (\phi_{pos} - \phi_{neg}) - T \frac{dU}{dT} \right] \]

In , the first term inside the brackets corresponds to irreversible (overpotential) heat generation, while the term \(T \frac{dU}{dT}\) represents the reversible (entropic) contribution. This formulation is consistent with Bernardi-type heat generation models and links the electrochemical and thermal behavior of the system through the current transfer rate .

Material properties

The thermophysical properties employed in the model are summarized in . The active jelly-roll region exhibits anisotropic thermal behavior, with conductivity defined as \( \mathbf{k} = (k_r, k_{\theta}, k_z) \), leading to enhanced heat conduction in the tangential and axial directions compared to the radial direction.

The positive and negative tabs are modeled as aluminium and steel, respectively, with high electrical conductivities that enable efficient current collection. In contrast, the active region is assumed to have negligible electrical conductivity, as commonly adopted in the MSMD formulation.

Initial and Boundary Conditions

The reference study evaluates the electrothermal response of the cell under constant-current discharges of 0.5C, 1C, and 1.5C, as well as under a variable-current HWFET driving cycle. In the variable-current case, the current profile was scaled to a maximum of approximately 5.5 A.

Thermally, the study assumes natural convection at the external surface and includes radiative exchange. The external radiation temperature is taken equal to the ambient temperature. The reported heat transfer coefficients increase with discharge rate, and radiation contributes at the same order as convection under free convection conditions.

  • Current-driven discharge cases: 0.5C, 1C, 1.5C, and HWFET
  • Ambient temperature imposed externally
  • Natural convection plus thermal radiation at the cell surface
  • Initial fields set from the fully charged initial condition

Numerical Implementation

In the original reference study, the NTGK model was solved within an MSMD framework, where the dual-potential equations and the thermal equation are coupled in time. For the present FreeFEM documentation, the same logic is useful as a roadmap: define separated regions, assign anisotropic thermal properties to the active zone, solve the electric potentials in the tab domains, compute the interfacial transfer rate \(j\), and assemble the thermal problem with the electrochemical source term \(q_{ech}\).

A mesh independence study for the NTGK case selected a mesh with 38,777 elements as a good compromise between accuracy and cost. In the reference simulations, a time step of 1 s was used for all cases, including 0.5C, 1C, 1.5C, and the HWFET current profile.

  • Region-based mesh for positive tab, negative tab, and active jelly-roll
  • Finite element spaces for temperature and electric potentials
  • Time-marching solution with electrothermal coupling at every step
  • Temperature-dependent NTGK closure through \(U(DoD,T)\) and \(Y(DoD,T)\)
  • Automatic computation of electrochemical heat generation from the NTGK source term

Implementation direction in FreeFEM: one practical route is to begin with a simplified two-dimensional section of the cylindrical cell, preserve the multi-region structure, and then progressively incorporate the full dual-potential coupling and voltage reconstruction.

Validation and Consistency

The reference study validates the models against measured surface temperature and reports RMS errors for each discharge condition. For the NTGK model, the reported RMS errors are:

  • 0.5C: 0.3 °C
  • 1C: 0.8 °C
  • 1.5C: 1.3 °C
  • HWFET: 0.3 °C

These values show that the NTGK model can reproduce the thermal response with good accuracy, especially at lower and moderate discharge rates. At the same time, the model is computationally much more demanding than lumped approaches, which is important when choosing the appropriate level of fidelity for engineering use.

Reported execution time: NTGK required 27120 s at 0.5C, 14280 s at 1C, 8989 s at 1.5C, and 26460 s for HWFET in the reported runs. This is one of the main trade-offs of the model: richer physics and voltage prediction at a significantly higher cost.

Results and Discussion

The NTGK framework is particularly useful here because it does not only return a temperature field. It also provides access to electrochemical heat generation trends and to the voltage response, making it a stronger documentation case than a purely thermal model.

Heat generation

In the reference comparison, the NTGK-predicted average volumetric heat generation was 7542 W/m3 at 0.5C, 22228 W/m3 at 1C, 52465 W/m3 at 1.5C, and 9861 W/m3 under the HWFET cycle. At constant current, the NTGK heat generation tends to rise near the beginning and the end of discharge, while under HWFET it follows the variability of the imposed current profile.

Temperature response

All three models compared in the paper remain consistent with the measured cell temperature, but the NTGK model shows the lowest RMS error at 0.5C and 1C. The most demanding case was 1.5C, where the measured temperature increase reached 18.1 °C. Under HWFET, all models remain in the same order of error and capture the overall dynamics well.

Voltage response

Another strong point of the NTGK model is that it reproduces the cell voltage together with the thermal response. The paper reports good agreement between measured and simulated voltage for the constant-current cases and for HWFET, although some discrepancy appears near end of discharge, where the minimum voltage is not fully reached.

Conclusions

This case now moves beyond a generic battery-modeling template and becomes a documented NTGK-oriented electrothermal study. The model is able to represent not only the thermal response of the cell, but also electrochemical heat generation and voltage evolution, which makes it especially attractive for engineering studies where thermal behavior alone is not sufficient.

The reported comparison shows that NTGK can achieve strong agreement with measured temperature, with its best thermal performance at 0.5C and 1C, while still remaining useful under the variable-current HWFET cycle. Its main limitation is computational cost, which is considerably higher than reduced thermal models.

For this documentation space, the next logical step is to translate this structure into a FreeFEM implementation: first preserving the region decomposition and coupled fields, then progressively refining the source-term evaluation, voltage reconstruction, and parameter calibration strategy.
